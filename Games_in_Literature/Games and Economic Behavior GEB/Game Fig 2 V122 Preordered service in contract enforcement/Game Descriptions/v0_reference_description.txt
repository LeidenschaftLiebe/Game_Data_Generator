set a concrete V0 instance with:
w = 2
c = 0.2
alpha = 3/4
p = 1/2

So the explicit outcomes become:
Not Enter: 1, 1
Honor: 2, 2
Enforce: 2, 1.8
Not Enforce: 0, 4

An investor first decides whether to NotEnter or Enter.
If the investor chooses NotEnter, the interaction ends there.
This gives 1 to the investor, and 1 to the entrepreneur.

If the investor chooses Enter, a random event occurs next.
With probability 1/4, the contract is BreachedExogenously.
With probability 3/4, it is NotBreached.

If the contract is NotBreached, the entrepreneur chooses between Honor and NotHonor.
If the entrepreneur chooses Honor, this gives 2 to the investor, and 2 to the entrepreneur.

If the entrepreneur chooses NotHonor, the court process begins.
The judiciary then Enforces with probability 1/2, or DoesNotEnforce with probability 1/2.
If the judiciary Enforces, this gives 2 to the investor, and 1.8 to the entrepreneur.
If the judiciary DoesNotEnforce, this gives 0 to the investor, and 4 to the entrepreneur.

If the contract is BreachedExogenously, the entrepreneur gets no choice and the court process begins immediately.
The judiciary then Enforces with probability 1/2, or DoesNotEnforce with probability 1/2.
If the judiciary Enforces, this gives 2 to the investor, and 1.8 to the entrepreneur.
If the judiciary DoesNotEnforce, this gives 0 to the investor, and 4 to the entrepreneur.

The paper studies this kind of contract-enforcement setting and then extends it by allowing a random breach before the entrepreneur has a chance to decide.
