Game descriptions for when player 1 = action 1, then player 2 loses with either action; 
when player 1 chooses action 2, player 2 wins either way, but action 1 results in a bigger win.
V1 - V10: 

1. Debater vs. Opponent

A debater (Player 1) chooses to argue Economics or Foreign policy. The opponent (Player 2) chooses their response line.

If Economics is chosen, the opponent always loses, whether they counter with Taxes (loss for Opponent) or Jobs (loss for Opponent).

If Foreign policy is chosen, the opponent always wins, though Countering with Diplomacy (better win for Opponent) is stronger than Countering with Defense (weaker win for Opponent).

Zero-sum: the opponent’s gain is the debater’s loss.






2. Lawyer vs. Prosecutor

A defense lawyer (Player 1) frames the case around Character evidence or Alibi evidence. The prosecutor (Player 2) replies.

If Character evidence is used, the prosecutor always fails, whether attacking with Past behavior (loss for Prosecutor) or Witness doubt (loss for Prosecutor).

If Alibi evidence is used, the prosecutor always succeeds, though Attacking the Timeline (better win for Prosecutor) is stronger than Attacking the Witness (weaker win for Prosecutor).

Zero-sum: courtroom victories swing one way or the other.






3. Candidate vs. Rival

A candidate (Player 1) campaigns on Education or Healthcare. The rival (Player 2) chooses their counter-issue.

If Education is chosen, the rival always loses, whether pushing Funding (loss for Rival) or Curriculum (loss for Rival).

If Healthcare is chosen, the rival always wins, though Pushing Costs (better win for Rival) is stronger than Pushing Access (weaker win for Rival).

Zero-sum: campaign momentum is a direct contest.






4. Chess Player vs. Opponent

A chess player (Player 1) opens with Queen’s Gambit or King’s Pawn. The opponent (Player 2) replies.

If Queen’s Gambit is played, the opponent always loses, whether defending with d5 (loss for Opponent) or e6 (loss for Opponent).

If King’s Pawn is played, the opponent always wins, though c5 Sicilian (better win for Opponent) is stronger than e5 (weaker win for Opponent).

Zero-sum: each move reshapes the balance.






5. Student vs. Examiner

A student (Player 1) chooses to answer Essay Question A or Essay Question B. The examiner (Player 2) selects grading focus.

If Question A is answered, the examiner always loses, whether emphasizing Structure (loss for Examiner) or Content (loss for Examiner).

If Question B is answered, the examiner always wins, though focusing on Critical thinking (better win for Examiner) beats focusing on Grammar (weaker win for Examiner).

Zero-sum: grading strength flips with the student’s choice.






6. Negotiator vs. Counterparty

A negotiator (Player 1) anchors the deal with High price or Low price. The counterparty (Player 2) reacts.

If High price is set, the counterparty always loses, whether they respond with Delay (loss for Counterparty) or Concede (loss for Counterparty).

If Low price is set, the counterparty always wins, though Demanding Extras (better win for Counterparty) is stronger than Accepting Quickly (weaker win for Counterparty).

Zero-sum: one side’s leverage erodes the other’s.






7. Journalist vs. Politician

A journalist (Player 1) asks about Corruption or Policy record. The politician (Player 2) answers.

If asked about Corruption, the politician always loses, whether answering with Denial (loss for Politician) or Deflection (loss for Politician).

If asked about Policy record, the politician always wins, though Highlighting Achievements (better win for Politician) is stronger than Shifting Blame (weaker win for Politician).

Zero-sum: every gain in narrative control is the other’s loss.






8. Teacher vs. Student

A teacher (Player 1) quizzes on History or Math. The student (Player 2) responds.

If the quiz is on History, the student always loses, whether answering with Dates (loss for Student) or Events (loss for Student).

If the quiz is on Math, the student always wins, though Solving Algebra (better win for Student) is stronger than Solving Geometry (weaker win for Student).

Zero-sum: knowledge advantage shifts directly between teacher and student.






9. Manager vs. Employee

A manager (Player 1) assigns tasks on Strategy or Execution. The employee (Player 2) reacts.

If assigned Strategy, the employee always loses, whether responding with Plans (loss for Employee) or Reports (loss for Employee).

If assigned Execution, the employee always wins, though Quick delivery (better win for Employee) is stronger than Minimal effort (weaker win for Employee).

Zero-sum: productivity wins come at the other’s expense.






10. Diplomat vs. Negotiator

A diplomat (Player 1) emphasizes Trade or Security. The negotiator (Player 2) counters.

If Trade is emphasized, the negotiator always loses, whether pushing Tariffs (loss for Negotiator) or Quotas (loss for Negotiator).

If Security is emphasized, the negotiator always wins, though Arms control (better win for Negotiator) is stronger than Border patrol (weaker win for Negotiator).

Zero-sum: diplomatic concessions move strictly one way.
