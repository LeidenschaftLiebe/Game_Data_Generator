Game descriptions for matching actions (match is a win for player 2 and mismatch is a win for player 1). 

V11 - V20: 

11. Detective vs. Thief

A thief plans a burglary while a detective sets up surveillance.

The thief can strike the jewelry shop or the art gallery.
The detective can stake out the jewelry shop or stake out the art gallery.

If the thief targets the jewelry shop, the detective wins by staking out the shop, but loses if staking out the gallery.

If the thief targets the art gallery, the detective wins by staking out the gallery, but loses if staking out the shop.






12. Fisherman vs. Poacher

A fisherman reports illegal fishing, while the poacher decides where to operate.

The poacher can fish at the lake or the river.
The fisherman can check the lake or check the river.

If the poacher goes to the lake, the fisherman wins by checking the lake, but loses if checking the river.

If the poacher goes to the river, the fisherman wins by checking the river, but loses if checking the lake.






13. Soldier vs. Sniper

A soldier advances across terrain while a sniper lies in wait.

The soldier can move through the forest or the ruins.
The sniper can aim at the forest or aim at the ruins.

If the soldier advances through the forest, the sniper wins by aiming at the forest, but loses if aiming at the ruins.

If the soldier advances through the ruins, the sniper wins by aiming at the ruins, but loses if aiming at the forest.






14. Pilot vs. Missile Operator

A fighter pilot dodges missiles as the operator decides where to fire.

The pilot can fly to the east sector or the west sector.
The operator can target the east sector or target the west sector.

If the pilot heads east, the operator wins by targeting east, but loses if targeting west.

If the pilot heads west, the operator wins by targeting west, but loses if targeting east.






15. Chess Player vs. Opponent

In a chess match, one player plans an opening, while the opponent prepares a defense.

The first player can open with the King’s pawn or the Queen’s pawn.
The opponent can defend against King’s pawn or defend against Queen’s pawn.

If the opening is King’s pawn, the opponent wins by defending against it, but loses if defending against Queen’s pawn.

If the opening is Queen’s pawn, the opponent wins by defending against it, but loses if defending against King’s pawn.






16. Student vs. Examiner

A student selects a topic to present, while the examiner decides what to focus grading on.

The student can present on History or Science.
The examiner can grade History or grade Science.

If the student chooses History, the examiner wins by grading History, but loses if grading Science.

If the student chooses Science, the examiner wins by grading Science, but loses if grading History.






17. Driver vs. Toll Inspector

A driver carrying contraband approaches a toll, and the inspector chooses which lane to monitor.

The driver can enter the left lane or the right lane.
The inspector can inspect the left lane or inspect the right lane.

If the driver uses the left lane, the inspector wins by inspecting left, but loses if inspecting right.

If the driver uses the right lane, the inspector wins by inspecting right, but loses if inspecting left.






18. Explorer vs. Rival

Two explorers race to claim territory, with one setting the path and the other blocking.

The explorer can head to the oasis or the mountain pass.
The rival can block the oasis or block the mountain pass.

If the explorer chooses the oasis, the rival wins by blocking the oasis, but loses if blocking the pass.

If the explorer chooses the pass, the rival wins by blocking the pass, but loses if blocking the oasis.






19. Athlete vs. Referee

An athlete prepares a trick play, while the referee positions to watch closely.

The athlete can execute a left-side play or a right-side play.
The referee can watch the left side or watch the right side.

If the athlete uses the left-side play, the referee wins by watching left, but loses if watching right.

If the athlete uses the right-side play, the referee wins by watching right, but loses if watching left.






20. Journalist vs. Politician

A journalist presses for answers, and the politician decides where to prepare.

The journalist can ask about foreign policy or domestic policy.
The politician can prepare for foreign policy or prepare for domestic policy.

If the journalist asks about foreign policy, the politician wins if prepared for foreign policy, but loses if prepared for domestic policy.

If the journalist asks about domestic policy, the politician wins if prepared for domestic policy, but loses if prepared for foreign policy.
