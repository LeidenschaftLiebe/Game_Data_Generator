V0 — Procedural Reasoning Is Not Lexicographic

This example is used to show that the order of the reasoning procedure matters, and that the process is not simply lexicographic.

Alice moves first and chooses a1 or a2.

Then Bob moves without knowing whether Alice chose a1 or a2, and Bob chooses b1 or b2.

If Alice chose a1 and Bob chose b1, the interaction ends there.
Alice gets 1, Bob gets 1, Emma gets 0, and David gets 0.

If Alice chose a2, then after Bob chooses b1 or b2, the move passes to Emma.
Emma does not know which of Bob’s two choices led to her move, and she chooses c1 or c2.

If the path is a2 then b2 then c1, everyone gets 0.
If the path is a2 then b2 then c2, Alice gets 0, Bob gets 0, Emma gets 1, and David gets 0.

If the path is a2 then b1 then c1, Alice gets 0, Bob gets 1, Emma gets 1, and David gets 0.

David moves in two cases: after a1 then b2, or after a2 then b1 then c2.
He does not know which of these two cases has occurred, and he chooses d1 or d2.

If David is reached after a1 then b2, then whether he chooses d1 or d2, Alice gets 1, Bob gets 0, Emma gets 0, and David gets 0.

If David is reached after a2 then b1 then c2, then whether he chooses d1 or d2, Alice gets 0, Bob gets 1, Emma gets 0, and David gets 0.
