This figure is used in the paper as a small two-player example showing that one well-known way of adding tiny mistakes 
does not recover every quasi-perfect equilibrium, even in a two-player setting.

Player 1 moves first and chooses between A, B, and E.

If Player 1 chooses E, the game ends immediately.
This gives 3 to Player 1, and 0 to Player 2.

If Player 1 chooses B, then Player 2 chooses between a and b.
If Player 2 chooses a, this gives 0 to Player 1, and 0 to Player 2.
If Player 2 chooses b, this gives 1 to Player 1, and 1 to Player 2.

If Player 1 chooses A, then Player 1 moves again and chooses between C and D.

If Player 1 chooses C, then Player 2 chooses between a and b.
If Player 2 chooses a, this gives 4 to Player 1, and 0 to Player 2.
If Player 2 chooses b, this gives 2 to Player 1, and 0 to Player 2.

If Player 1 chooses D, then Player 2 chooses between a and b.
If Player 2 chooses a, this gives 1 to Player 1, and 1 to Player 2.
If Player 2 chooses b, this gives 0 to Player 1, and 0 to Player 2.

When Player 2 makes the choice between a and b, Player 2 cannot tell whether the situation came from A then C, from A then D, or directly from B.
That hidden detail is exactly what matters in the paper’s discussion.
