This is the paper’s baseline gradual game.
It is an infinitely repeated trust setting with a sender and a receiver.
In each period, the sender first chooses one of four trust levels: N, L, M, or H.
If the sender chooses a positive trust level, the receiver then chooses between R and D.

The paper explains that N means no trust.
The positive trust levels are low trust L, medium trust M, and high trust H.
The baseline gradual and binary game trees are shown in Fig. 1 of the article.

In this one-shot tree, the sender moves first.

If the sender chooses N, the interaction ends immediately.
This gives the sender 24, and the receiver 15.

If the sender chooses L, the receiver chooses between R and D.
If the receiver chooses R, this gives the sender 32, and the receiver 22.
If the receiver chooses D, this gives the sender 20, and the receiver 42.

If the sender chooses M, the receiver again chooses between R and D.
If the receiver chooses R, this gives the sender 44, and the receiver 36.
If the receiver chooses D, this gives the sender 12, and the receiver 72.

If the sender chooses H, the receiver again chooses between R and D.
If the receiver chooses R, this gives the sender 56, and the receiver 42.
If the receiver chooses D, this gives the sender 0, and the receiver 80.

The article uses this gradual version to study whether starting small can help build trust under asymmetric information, 
and compares it to a simpler binary version that keeps only N and H.
