This figure comes from the paper as a small example where one player later has to act again 
without being able to tell exactly how that point was reached.
The article uses it to show why standard backward-induction style reasoning becomes tricky in such settings.

Nature first chooses which side of the tree is used, with probability 1/2 for each side.

On the left side, Player 2 moves first.
Player 2 chooses between OUT and IN.

If Player 2 chooses OUT, the game ends there.
This gives Player 1 1, and Player 2 -1.

If Player 2 chooses IN, then Player 1 moves at the upper node and chooses between S and D.
If Player 1 chooses S, this gives Player 1 -2, and Player 2 2.
If Player 1 chooses D, this gives Player 1 2, and Player 2 -2.

On the right side, Player 1 moves first.
Player 1 chooses between Out and In.

If Player 1 chooses Out, the game ends there.
This gives Player 1 -1, and Player 2 1.

If Player 1 chooses In, then Player 1 moves again at the upper node and chooses between S and D.
If Player 1 chooses S, this gives Player 1 2, and Player 2 -2.
If Player 1 chooses D, this gives Player 1 -2, and Player 2 2.

The two upper Player 1 nodes are linked.
So when Player 1 makes that upper choice between S and D, 
Player 1 cannot tell whether the path came from the left side after Player 2 chose IN, 
or from the right side after Player 1 earlier chose In.
