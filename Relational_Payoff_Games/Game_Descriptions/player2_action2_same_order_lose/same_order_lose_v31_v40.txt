Game descriptions for when player 1 = action 1, then player 2 loses with either action; 
when player 1 chooses action 2, player 2 wins either way, but action 1 results in a bigger win.
V31 - V40: 

31. Candidate vs. Opponent

A candidate (Player 1) stresses Foreign policy or Healthcare. The opponent (Player 2) crafts rebuttals.

If on Foreign policy, the opponent always loses, whether rebutting with Military readiness (loss for Opponent) or Alliances (loss for Opponent).

If on Healthcare, the opponent always wins, though Insurance costs (better win for Opponent) is stronger than Hospital access (weaker win for Opponent).

Zero-sum: the campaign swing is absolute.






32. Mentor vs. Apprentice

A mentor (Player 1) teaches Crafting or Strategy. The apprentice (Player 2) responds.

If Crafting is taught, the apprentice always loses, whether learning Tools (loss for Apprentice) or Materials (loss for Apprentice).

If Strategy is taught, the apprentice always wins, though focusing on Planning (better win for Apprentice) is stronger than Tactics (weaker win for Apprentice).

Zero-sum: learning gains are strictly opposed.






33. Author vs. Editor

An author (Player 1) drafts Poetry or Prose. The editor (Player 2) reacts.

If Poetry is drafted, the editor always loses, whether critiquing Meter (loss for Editor) or Imagery (loss for Editor).

If Prose is drafted, the editor always wins, though Grammar edits (better win for Editor) are stronger than Stylistic edits (weaker win for Editor).

Zero-sum: the editor’s power grows as the author’s shrinks.






34. Leader vs. Advisor

A leader (Player 1) bases policy on Security or Economy. The advisor (Player 2) reacts.

If on Security, the advisor always loses, whether suggesting Defense budget (loss for Advisor) or Border patrol (loss for Advisor).

If on Economy, the advisor always wins, though stressing Tax reform (better win for Advisor) is stronger than stressing Jobs plan (weaker win for Advisor).

Zero-sum: policy leverage belongs fully to one.






35. Actor vs. Director

An actor (Player 1) insists on Ad-libbing or Sticking to script. The director (Player 2) responds.

If Ad-libbing is chosen, the director always loses, whether responding with Retakes (loss for Director) or Notes (loss for Director).

If Sticking to script is chosen, the director always wins, though Blocking adjustments (better win for Director) are stronger than Tone suggestions (weaker win for Director).

Zero-sum: creative control flows entirely to one.






36. Lawyer vs. Jury

A lawyer (Player 1) emphasizes Character defense or Timeline defense. The jury (Player 2) weighs evidence.

If Character defense is used, the jury always loses, whether doubting Credibility (loss for Jury) or questioning Consistency (loss for Jury).

If Timeline defense is used, the jury always wins, though Precise dates (better win for Jury) are stronger than Approximate times (weaker win for Jury).

Zero-sum: verdict weight falls wholly to one side.






37. Negotiator vs. Delegate

A negotiator (Player 1) opens talks on Trade or Security. The delegate (Player 2) replies.

If on Trade, the delegate always loses, whether countering with Tariffs (loss for Delegate) or Quotas (loss for Delegate).

If on Security, the delegate always wins, though focusing on Alliances (better win for Delegate) is stronger than focusing on Arms (weaker win for Delegate).

Zero-sum: bargaining advantage moves completely.






38. Student vs. Examiner

A student (Player 1) answers Theory question or Practical question. The examiner (Player 2) grades.

If answering Theory, the examiner always loses, whether grading on Concepts (loss for Examiner) or Definitions (loss for Examiner).

If answering Practical, the examiner always wins, though grading on Method (better win for Examiner) is stronger than grading on Result (weaker win for Examiner).

Zero-sum: exam balance lies wholly with one.






39. Politician vs. Interviewer

A politician (Player 1) chooses to discuss Education or Foreign affairs. The interviewer (Player 2) probes.

If on Education, the interviewer always loses, whether probing Funding (loss for Interviewer) or Standards (loss for Interviewer).

If on Foreign affairs, the interviewer always wins, though asking about Allies (better win for Interviewer) is stronger than asking about Defense (weaker win for Interviewer).

Zero-sum: questioning power shifts entirely.






40. Author vs. Reviewer

An author (Player 1) releases a Memoir or a Novel. The reviewer (Player 2) critiques.

If the Memoir is released, the reviewer always loses, whether critiquing Tone (loss for Reviewer) or Detail (loss for Reviewer).

If the Novel is released, the reviewer always wins, though Plot critique (better win for Reviewer) is stronger than Style critique (weaker win for Reviewer).

Zero-sum: critical reception flips decisively.
