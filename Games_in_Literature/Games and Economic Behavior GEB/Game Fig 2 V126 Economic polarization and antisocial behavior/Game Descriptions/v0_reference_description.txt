This is the Risky exit treatment from the paper’s Inequality Game.
The two roles are a Strong player and a Weak player.
In the risky version, their main choices are made at the same time, 
and the paper adds an exit option that can be used before the encounter produces any surplus. 
If at least one player exits, both earn 0, 0.

The Weak player chooses between Exploit, Exit, and Collaborate.
At the same time, the Strong player chooses between Exit, Defensive, and Generous.
The three Strong decision nodes are linked because the Strong player does not know which of the three Weak actions was chosen when making that choice.

If the Weak player chooses Exploit and the Strong player chooses Exit, both get 0, 0.
If the Weak player chooses Exploit and the Strong player chooses Defensive, this gives the Weak player 0, and the Strong player 10.
If the Weak player chooses Exploit and the Strong player chooses Generous, this gives the Weak player 9, and the Strong player 1.

If the Weak player chooses Exit, the result is always 0, 0, no matter what the Strong player chose.
So Exit destroys all the surplus for both sides.

If the Weak player chooses Collaborate and the Strong player chooses Exit, both get 0, 0.
If the Weak player chooses Collaborate and the Strong player chooses Defensive, this gives the Weak player 1, and the Strong player 9.
If the Weak player chooses Collaborate and the Strong player chooses Generous, this gives the Weak player 5, and the Strong player 5.

The paper uses this environment to compare what happens when generosity is risky for the Strong player versus when it is safe, 
and studies whether disappointed expectations can trigger antisocial exit behavior.
