1) Bottled Water (Stackelberg)

ClearSprings decides first whether to produce a large or small quantity of bottled water for the upcoming season.
BluePeak observes that choice and then decides its own batch size.
When both companies produce large quantities, competition pushes prices down and each earns $800 thousand.
If ClearSprings expands production while BluePeak holds back, ClearSprings dominates the market and earns $1.6 million, while BluePeak makes $800 thousand.
If ClearSprings stays conservative and BluePeak expands, BluePeak earns $1.6 million while ClearSprings makes $800 thousand.
When both keep production small, prices remain stable and each earns $1.2 million.






2) Cement Supply (Stackelberg)

MetroCem, the larger cement producer, sets its output for the quarter before UrbanMix announces its own production plan.
UrbanMix observes MetroCem’s decision and reacts accordingly.
When both firms ramp up production, prices fall and each earns $6.4 million.
If MetroCem produces aggressively while UrbanMix remains cautious, MetroCem captures more of the market and earns $12.8 million, while UrbanMix earns $6.4 million.
If MetroCem limits production and UrbanMix expands, the roles reverse.
If both remain conservative, the market price stays strong and each earns $9.6 million.






3) Airline Routes (Stackelberg)

SkyLift decides first whether to add many flights or keep a limited schedule on a popular regional route.
AirNova observes this move and then sets its own frequency.
When both add many flights, fares drop and both airlines end up with $15 million.
If SkyLift expands while AirNova stays limited, SkyLift earns $28 million, and AirNova’s profit remains at $15 million.
If SkyLift stays limited and AirNova expands, AirNova earns $28 million, while SkyLift earns $15 million.
When both stay limited, they maintain high fares and each earns $22 million.
