Game descriptions for when player 1 = action 1, then player 2 wins with either action; 
when player 1 chooses action 2, player 2 loses either way, but action 1 results in a smaller loss.
V31 - V40: 

31. Prisoner vs. Guard

A prisoner plans an escape, while the guard chooses which exit to watch.

The prisoner can run to the north gate or the south tunnel.
The guard can watch the north gate or watch the south tunnel.

If the north gate is chosen, the guard always catches the prisoner, no matter which exit is watched.

If the south tunnel is chosen, the guard always fails. Watching the north gate limits the damage, while watching the south tunnel makes the escape cleaner.






32. Scientist vs. Experiment Failure

A scientist sets up an experiment, while hidden flaws may ruin it.

The scientist can test with the blue compound or the red compound.
The failure can occur in the blue setup or the red setup.

If the blue compound is used, the scientist always succeeds, no matter where the flaw lies.

If the red compound is used, the scientist always fails. A flaw in the blue setup reduces the failure, while a flaw in the red setup makes it disastrous.






33. Soldier vs. Minefield

A soldier advances into hostile terrain, while mines are buried.

The soldier can march through the clearing or the gully.
The mines can be placed in the clearing or in the gully.

If the clearing is chosen, the soldier always gets through safely, whichever spot is mined.

If the gully is chosen, the soldier always suffers. Mines in the clearing cause less harm, while mines in the gully cause far greater harm.






34. Pilot vs. Storm

A pilot chooses a flight route, while a storm shifts.

The pilot can fly over the plains or across the mountains.
The storm can hit the plains or the mountains.

If the plains are chosen, the pilot always avoids trouble, no matter where the storm breaks.

If the mountains are chosen, the pilot always struggles. A storm on the plains is less dangerous, while a storm in the mountains is devastating.






35. Archer vs. Target Trick

An archer competes in a contest, while targets are rigged with tricks.

The archer can shoot at the inner circle or the outer ring.
The trick can affect the inner circle or the outer ring.

If the inner circle is chosen, the archer always scores well, regardless of where the trick is placed.

If the outer ring is chosen, the archer always fails. A trick in the inner circle makes the loss smaller, while a trick in the outer ring makes it worse.






36. Captain vs. Pirate

A captain sails with treasure, while a pirate sets an ambush.

The captain can sail along the strait or the open sea.
The pirate can ambush the strait or ambush the open sea.

If the strait is chosen, the pirate always seizes the treasure, no matter where he lies in wait.

If the open sea is chosen, the pirate always misses. Ambushing the strait reduces his loss, while ambushing the open sea leaves him empty-handed.






37. Athlete vs. Weather Judge

An outdoor athlete competes while judges decide how to score under changing weather.

The athlete can compete in long jump or high jump.
The weather can favor the long jump or the high jump.

If long jump is chosen, the athlete always does well, no matter the weather.

If high jump is chosen, the athlete always performs poorly. Favorable weather for long jump softens the failure, while favorable weather for high jump makes it harsher.






38. Commander vs. Saboteur

A commander sends supplies, while a saboteur tries to strike.

The commander can send them by the railroad or the convoy.
The saboteur can attack the railroad or attack the convoy.

If the railroad is used, the saboteur always succeeds, regardless of which target he chooses.

If the convoy is used, the saboteur always fails. Striking the railroad makes it a smaller failure, while striking the convoy makes it a bigger one.






39. Judge vs. Lawyer

A judge calls a case type, while the lawyer prepares arguments.

The judge can open with civil law or criminal law.
The lawyer can prepare civil law or prepare criminal law.

If civil law is called, the lawyer always shines, whichever arguments are chosen.

If criminal law is called, the lawyer always falters. Preparing civil law makes it less damaging, while preparing criminal law makes it worse.






40. Thief vs. Guard Dog

A thief tries to sneak in, while the guard dog roams the yard.

The thief can slip by the front fence or the side gate.
The dog can patrol the front fence or the side gate.

If the front fence is chosen, the dog always stops the thief, whichever patrol is taken.

If the side gate is chosen, the dog always fails. Patrolling the front fence limits the loss, while patrolling the side gate makes it worse.
