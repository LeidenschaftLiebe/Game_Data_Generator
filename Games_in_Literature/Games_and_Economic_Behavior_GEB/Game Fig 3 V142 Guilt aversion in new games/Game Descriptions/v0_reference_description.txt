This figure is the article’s Donation game.
It is one of the three-player quasi-trust mini-games used to study whether guilt depends on another player’s vulnerability.

There are three players: A, B, and C.
Player A moves first and chooses between Out and In.

If A chooses Out, the game ends immediately.
The result is 170 for A, 100 for B, and 30 for C.

If A chooses In, A sends 25 ECU from A’s own endowment to B.
Then B chooses between Left and Right.
In the article’s wording, the flow after In is A -> B -> C.

If B chooses Left, B keeps 20 ECU and transfers 5 ECU to C.
That transferred amount is doubled, so C receives 10.
The result is 145 for A, 120 for B, and 40 for C.

If B chooses Right, B transfers the full 25 ECU to C.
That amount is doubled, so C receives 50.
The result is 145 for A, 100 for B, and 80 for C.

In this game, the article says that A is vulnerable because A’s own endowment is the one put at risk.
C is vulnerable because C’s final amount depends on B’s later choice.
