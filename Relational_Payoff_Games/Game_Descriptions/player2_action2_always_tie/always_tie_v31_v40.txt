Game descriptions for when player 1 = action 1, then player 2 loses with either action and the losses are the same; 
when player 1 chooses action 2, player 2 wins either way, and the rewards are the same.
V31 - V40: 

31. Candidate vs. Opponent

A candidate (Player 1) stresses Foreign policy or Healthcare. The opponent (Player 2) crafts rebuttals.

If the topic is Foreign policy, the opponent always loses. Rebutting with Military readiness or Alliances both lead to the same failure.

If the topic is Healthcare, the opponent always wins. Focusing on Insurance costs or Hospital access both produce the same success.

Zero-sum: the campaign swing is absolute.






32. Mentor vs. Apprentice

A mentor (Player 1) teaches Crafting or Strategy. The apprentice (Player 2) responds.

If Crafting is taught, the apprentice always loses. Practicing Tools or Materials both end in the same failure.

If Strategy is taught, the apprentice always wins. Studying Planning or Tactics both result in the same success.

Zero-sum: learning gains are strictly opposed.






33. Author vs. Editor

An author (Player 1) drafts Poetry or Prose. The editor (Player 2) critiques.

If Poetry is drafted, the editor always loses. Critiquing Meter or Imagery both result in the same losing outcome.

If Prose is drafted, the editor always wins. Editing Grammar or Style both lead to the same winning result.

Zero-sum: editorial power grows as author control shrinks.






34. Leader vs. Advisor

A leader (Player 1) bases policy on Security or Economy. The advisor (Player 2) reacts.

If the focus is Security, the advisor always loses. Recommending Defense budget or Border patrol both result in the same defeat.

If the focus is Economy, the advisor always wins. Suggesting Tax reform or Jobs plan both deliver the same win.

Zero-sum: policy leverage lies wholly with one.






35. Actor vs. Director

An actor (Player 1) insists on Ad-libbing or Sticking to script. The director (Player 2) responds.

If Ad-libbing is chosen, the director always loses. Responding with Retakes or Notes both bring the same losing result.

If Sticking to script is chosen, the director always wins. Adjusting Blocking or Tone both lead to the same successful outcome.

Zero-sum: creative control flows entirely to one.






36. Lawyer vs. Jury

A lawyer (Player 1) emphasizes Character defense or Timeline defense. The jury (Player 2) weighs evidence.

If Character defense is used, the jury always loses. Questioning Credibility or Consistency both lead to the same failure.

If Timeline defense is used, the jury always wins. Highlighting Precise dates or Approximate times both bring the same success.

Zero-sum: verdict weight falls wholly to one side.






37. Negotiator vs. Delegate

A negotiator (Player 1) opens talks on Trade or Security. The delegate (Player 2) replies.

If Trade is the subject, the delegate always loses. Countering with Tariffs or Quotas both produce the same loss.

If Security is the subject, the delegate always wins. Stressing Alliances or Arms both yield the same winning outcome.

Zero-sum: bargaining advantage moves completely.






38. Student vs. Examiner

A student (Player 1) answers a Theory question or a Practical question. The examiner (Player 2) grades.

If the answer is on Theory, the examiner always loses. Grading Concepts or Definitions both end in the same defeat.

If the answer is on Practical, the examiner always wins. Grading Method or Result both bring the same success.

Zero-sum: exam advantage rests with one.






39. Politician vs. Interviewer

A politician (Player 1) discusses Education or Foreign affairs. The interviewer (Player 2) probes.

If on Education, the interviewer always loses. Asking about Funding or Standards both produce the same failure.

If on Foreign affairs, the interviewer always wins. Asking about Allies or Defense both lead to the same winning outcome.

Zero-sum: questioning power shifts entirely.






40. Author vs. Reviewer

An author (Player 1) releases a Memoir or a Novel. The reviewer (Player 2) critiques.

If the Memoir is published, the reviewer always loses. Criticizing Tone or Detail both result in the same loss.

If the Novel is published, the reviewer always wins. Critiquing Plot or Style both produce the same success.

Zero-sum: critical reception flips decisively.
