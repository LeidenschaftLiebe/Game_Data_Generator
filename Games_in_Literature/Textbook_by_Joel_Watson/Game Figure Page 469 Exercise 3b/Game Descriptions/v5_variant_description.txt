V5 (cybersecurity dual-lead scenario)

At the start, the system is equally likely to fall under Lead A’s protocol or Lead B’s protocol.
If Lead A’s protocol is active, Lead A makes the first move.
If Lead B’s protocol is active, Lead B makes the first move.
The external responder does not know which lead is actually in control when choosing the final response.

Under Lead A’s protocol, Lead A chooses between an upper response and a lower response.
The external responder then chooses between L and R without knowing whether Lead A or Lead B is active.
If Lead A’s protocol and the upper response are followed by L, the final reward is 2 for Lead A, 0 for Lead B, and 2 for the responder.
If Lead A’s protocol and the upper response are followed by R, all three get 0.
If Lead A’s protocol and the lower response are followed by L, all three get 0.
If Lead A’s protocol and the lower response are followed by R, the final reward is 4 for Lead A, 0 for Lead B, and 4 for the responder.

Under Lead B’s protocol, Lead B chooses between an upper response and a lower response.
The external responder again chooses between L and R without knowing which lead is active.
If Lead B’s protocol and the upper response are followed by L, the final reward is 0 for Lead A, 0 for Lead B, and 2 for the responder.
If Lead B’s protocol and the upper response are followed by R, the final reward is 0 for Lead A, 2 for Lead B, and 0 for the responder.
If Lead B’s protocol and the lower response are followed by L, the final reward is 0 for Lead A, 4 for Lead B, and 0 for the responder.
If Lead B’s protocol and the lower response are followed by R, the final reward is 0 for Lead A, 0 for Lead B, and 4 for the responder.
