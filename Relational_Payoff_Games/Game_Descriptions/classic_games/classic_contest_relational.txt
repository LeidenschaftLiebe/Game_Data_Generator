1. Cournot Duopoly – “The Water Sellers”

Two water sellers draw from the same spring. 
Each must decide how many barrels to bring to the market. 
he more barrels each carries, the lower the price of water falls. 
Each seller’s profit depends not only on their own barrels, but also on how many the rival brings.






2. Bertrand Duopoly (Homogeneous Goods) – “The Gas Stations”

Two gas stations on the same road sell identical fuel. 
Each owner sets the price on their sign at the same time. 
Drivers flock to the cheaper station, and if both stations post the same price, they split the customers. 
The lower the price, the smaller the profit margin.






3. Stackelberg Duopoly – “The Apple Orchards”
Two orchard owners harvest apples to sell in town. 
The first farmer decides how many baskets to send to market. 
Seeing this, the second farmer then chooses how many baskets to send. 
The market price falls with the total baskets, but the first mover enjoys the advantage of setting the tone for the market.






4. Entry Deterrence (Capacity / Limit Pricing) – “The Brewery Guarding Its Market”
An established brewery controls the local beer trade. 
A new brewer considers opening shop. 
The established brewery can flood the market with barrels or cut prices so low that a newcomer could not make profit. 
If the threat works, the newcomer stays out; if not, competition begins.






5. War of Attrition / All-Pay – “The Mining Rights Battle”
Two companies want rights to a gold mine. 
Each pours money into legal fees, advertising, and lobbying. 
Both keep spending as long as they stay in the fight, but only one eventually secures the mine. 
The rival walks away empty-handed but still bears all the expenses.
