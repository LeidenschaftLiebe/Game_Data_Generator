This figure is the article’s Exploitation game.
It is one of the three-player quasi-trust mini-games used to study whether guilt depends on another player’s vulnerability.

There are three players: A, B, and C.
Player A moves first and chooses between Out and In.

If A chooses Out, the game ends immediately.
The result is 170 for A, 100 for B, and 30 for C.

If A chooses In, A causes 25 ECU to be taken from C’s endowment and handed to B.
Then B chooses between Left and Right.
In the article’s wording, the flow after In is C -> B -> A.

If B chooses Left, B keeps 20 ECU and transfers 5 ECU to A.
That transferred amount is doubled, so A receives 10.
The result is 180 for A, 120 for B, and 5 for C.

If B chooses Right, B transfers the full 25 ECU to A.
That amount is doubled, so A receives 50.
The result is 220 for A, 100 for B, and 5 for C.

In this game, the article says that A is vulnerable in terms of final amount only,
while C is vulnerable because C’s own endowment is the one put at risk.
